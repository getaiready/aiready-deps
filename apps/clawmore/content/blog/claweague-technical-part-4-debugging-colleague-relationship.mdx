# Technical: Debugging the Colleague Relationship

> Agentic systems are non-deterministic. Knowing **how** your colleague made a decision is more important than the decision itself.

---

## The "Black Box" Problem

The biggest fear in autonomous systems is the silent failure—an agent that creates a subtle bug because it misunderstood a requirement.

To fix this, we don't just look at the code. We look at the **Trace**.

## Observability: Inside the Agent's Mind

Every mutation performed by a **Claweague** is logged with a full "Reasoning Trace." You can see the planning, the tool execution, and the validation steps.

### Examining a Trace:

By analyzing the trace, you can find the "Cognitive Mismatch":

- Did the agent have the wrong context?
- Was the system prompt too vague?
- Was there a tool failure that wasn't handled correctly?

## Refining the Brief

Most "agent bugs" are actually **briefing bugs**. If your colleague is making mistakes, the first thing to check is the `sync-rules.json` or the skill's instructions.

**Pro Tip**: Use a "Feedback Claw" to review your own briefs before you send them to the primary agent.

## Atomic Mutations and Revertability

Because your Claweague works in a dedicated GitHub repository, every change is a Commit. If your colleague goes "rogue," you have a perfect audit trail and an invitation to `git revert`.

1. **Isolation**: Changes stay in specialized branches.
2. **Review**: Human-in-the-loop triggers a Pull Request.
3. **Rollback**: One-click reverts for unstable mutations.

## A Reliable Future

Debugging is no longer about finding a semicolon; it's about **aligning intent**. As you refine this relationship, the "friction" of collaboration drops, and the velocity of evolution skyrockets.

**The future of engineering isn't just writing code—it's managing the agents who do.**
